Fuel-buying minutes ???were only my notes???

Ex-Enemalta finance head lambasts Auditor???s conclusions

Enemalta’s former chief financial officer has admitted that hand­written papers shown in a damning audit report as minutes were his, but claimed they were just his own working notes.

Pippo Pandolfino lambasted the conclusions released earlier this month by Auditor General Anthony Mifsud and said neither he nor any of the former members of the committee were interviewed by the National Audit Office.

“Had they done so, perhaps the misunderstandings on their part would have been avoided much earlier,” Mr Pandolfino told Times of Malta.

Fuel Procurement Committee took no minutes during my time
- former chief finance officer

Mr Pandolfino attended all meetings of the corporation’s Fuel Procurement Committee (FPC) between 2004 and 2009. The NAO report was very critical of the way the FPC used to conduct its business between 2008 and 2010 and underlined the fact that the minutes of the meetings at the time “lacked the most rudi­mentary level of detail and bore no information relating to meeting discussions and the decision-making process”.

In its report, the NAO criticised the practices adopted by the FPC raising serious questions about lack of record keeping, proper testing for inferior quality fuel, and lack of proper records on who took decisions.

A copy of a handwritten, mostly illegible note, of one of the meetings, which according to the NAO was “a sample of FPC minutes”, was published in the report and was highlighted in the media.

However, it turns out this paper formed part of the personal notes of the chief financial officer (CFO).

Admitting that during his time on the FPC, no minutes were ever taken – as this was the custom adopted by the corporation – Mr Pandolfino took the NAO to task over this “flaw” since they were mistaking his working papers as being official minutes of the meetings.

‘No FPC secretary’

“The FPC did not have a secretary, I did not have the role of secretary and I was never asked to carry the role of secretary,” Mr Pandolfino stressed.

“The papers referred to by the Auditor as minutes were in fact not minutes but my working papers,” said Mr Pandolfino, who left the corporation in 2009 to join Island Bunker Oils as CEO.

According to the former CFO, the fuel procurement committee used to go through the salient financial features of the offers received “and I recorded these on my working papers”.

He explained that these sheets would be analysed to identify the most favourable offers and negotiations would then be conducted – viva-voce over speaker phone – on the basis of the best offers.

At the end of the meeting all documents were signed and placed in a sealed cabinet in the chairman’s office and no documentation was allowed to leave the office.

According to the NAO report, it was only from 2011 onwards that the FPC started keeping minutes of the meetings after the introduction of a new policy as soon as former minister Tonio Fenech took over the political responsibility of Enemalta from Austin Gatt.

Former members of the FPC between 2008 and 2010 confirmed to Times of Malta that the committee was always considered at the corporation as ‘Chairman’s territory’ and things had only started changing following the change of ministerial responsibility and the appointment of a new chairman after the departure of Alex Tranter from the helm of the corporation.

On his part, Mr Tranter has so far not replied to questions by Times of Malta on why he did not feel the need to keep formal minutes of the FPC.

He has only said that following publication of the NAO report he has passed on a statement to the AG office.

He also said he has authorised the Auditor General to publish this statement.

Mr Tranter said that fuel procurement decisions taken under his non-executive chairmanship “were all unanimous and all committee members present signed all the committee documents endorsing decisions taken”.
